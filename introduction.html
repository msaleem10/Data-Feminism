Introduction & System Analysis

Problem definition, data provenance, and power structures

Problem Overview

What is the problem? The United States financial disclosure system relies on a legally mandated 48 hour reporting delay that grants corporate insiders a temporal monopoly on market moving knowledge. By auditing over 1.7 million SEC Form 4 transactions this project examines how the algorithm of this Reported Silence functions as a structural mechanism for wealth extraction.

Why is it significant? Ethics rules nominally mandate transparency but the timing and format of this data are not neutral. They actively shape who can benefit from market shifts. Transparency without speed is merely a historical record of wealth extraction. The 48 hour delay is not a clerical buffer but a systemic tool that extracts retirement wealth by ensuring information only saturates the top.

Who does it affect? A two tiered information economy has emerged. On one side are the Data Elite including institutional hedge funds and high wealth insiders who weaponize perfect administrative compliance to reposition portfolios in the dark. On the other side is the American middle class whose 401k plans and pension funds operate on slower public facing cycles. The average citizen encounters financial data only after the fact absorbing the market volatility and mathematical aftershocks of elite capital flight.


Data Initiative and Algorithm Overview

This project focuses on the intersection of the primary federal disclosure algorithm and the counter data platforms that ingest its outputs. The baseline system is the Securities and Exchange Commission EDGAR database which serves as the official repository for insider transaction data.

What does it claim to do?

The SEC reporting system operates on a binary algorithmic logic based on a rigid 48 hour window where a trade is either on time or delayed. It claims to ensure market fairness and transparency by legally mandating that insiders disclose their transactions to the public.

Who uses it?

Corporate insiders author the filings. The SEC acts as a passive repository. Institutional actors and hedge funds use counter data platforms like Refinitiv LSEG to access the data instantly. Retail traders use platforms like Quiver Quantitative or OpenInsider to track trends.

Why does it matter?

It matters because analyzing these systems reveals how structural power is maintained. The system does not fix the inequality of the SEC delay. Institutional platforms commodify the delay for hedge funds while populist platforms gamify it for retail traders.


Data Provenance

Data Sources

  • Where did the data come from? The primary dataset consists of SEC Form 4 Non Derivative Transactions accessed via the EDGAR database.
  • Who collected it? The data is mandated and hosted by the federal government but authored entirely by corporate insiders.
  • When was it collected? The audit covers 1.7 million records organized quarterly from January 2020 to December 2025.
  • How was it collected? Data is collected through mandatory electronic self reported filings submitted by insiders to the SEC.

Data Control & Access

  • Who controls the data? The federal government maintains the repository but private capital controls the actionable data through high speed API access.
  • Who has access? The data is technically public but institutional actors pay for direct ultra low latency pipelines to bypass public friction.
  • What are the access restrictions? The state weaponizes format as a form of friction. Public data is siloed into fragmented quarterly tab separated TSV files requiring significant invisible labor to consolidate and analyze.

What's Missing?

Critically examining the absent data reveals systemic blind spots:

  • Point of entry verification is missing. The SEC database accepts glaring anomalies including reporting delays logged as occurring thousands of years in the past without triggering systemic alarms.
  • When researchers clean this data using standard error handling techniques corrupted date strings are dropped. This creates a survivorship bias that hides the most evasive and unaccountable actors.
  • The context or intent behind a trade is excluded flattening complex human behavior and situated knowledges into clinical metrics.


Power Structures Analysis

Examining how power operates within the financial disclosure ecosystem reveals a hierarchy optimized for institutional speed.

Institutional Power

The regulatory algorithm grants insiders a window of privacy to reposition their wealth before the public can react. The highest wealth insiders possess the highest rates of perfect compliance using expensive legal teams to master the algorithmic deadline and shield massive capital flights from regulatory scrutiny.

Economic Power

While the SEC data is publicly funded venture backed platforms and massive corporate subscriptions control its distribution. Platforms like Refinitiv monetize the reporting silence extracting profit by providing ultra low latency feeds to elite clients at the expense of slower public market participants.

Data Power

The government maintains the illusion of a public commons but the power to process and act on the data is restricted. High frequency counter data platforms extract the alpha from the market leaving retail investors and pension fund managers to absorb the mathematical aftershocks.

Narrative Power

The data creates an illusion of objective truth. It forces human behavior into a clean predictive binary hiding the messy unverified nature of self reported data behind proprietary mathematics. The narrative is framed around compliance while silencing the reality of wealth extraction.


Positionality Statement

Why does this matter to you? As a Math major and Data Science minor at Bryn Mawr College I am trained to treat data as something that can be cleaned coerced and modeled until it yields a reliable signal. In my quantitative coursework correctness is defined by mathematical convergence and statistical validity. In my journalism work it is defined by accuracy and sourcing. Both contexts however often operate under the assumption that once the numbers are right the work is done.

What is your relationship to this data or community? I cannot treat these numbers as abstract. The 1.7 million rows of transactions in this audit do not just represent corporate paperwork they represent the economic precarity of the working public. When insiders can convert advance knowledge of a market crash into private profit the ethics of information asymmetry becomes a lived reality for average people whose long term financial security is tied to the stock market.

How might your perspective shape your analysis? This project sits at the intersection of quantitative research and social justice. I share technical methods with analysts who search for alpha in SEC disclosure data but I align politically with those who use these techniques to expose concentrated power. Grounding this project in Data Feminism allows me to ask not only who profits from administrative delay but what a more just financial infrastructure might look like if we optimized for the protection of public wealth rather than the speed of elite capital.
