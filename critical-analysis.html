Critical Data Analysis

Data audit, visualization, and intersectional harm analysis

Data Availability & Methodology

Data Collection

How is the data collected? The data for this audit is sourced from the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) EDGAR database. Specifically, it relies on mandatory self-reported Form 4 filings, which corporate insiders must submit within two business days following a transaction. The data is publicly hosted but heavily siloed into fragmented, quarterly tab-separated (TSV) files spanning from 2006 to 2025.

What data is collected? The dataset captures transaction metadata, including the ACCESSION_NUMBER (unique identifier), TRANS_DATE (when the trade occurred), FILING_DATE (when the public was notified), transaction shares, price per share, and the insider's relationship to the company. My specific audit focuses on the timeline delta between the transaction and the filing.

Data Analysis & Use

How is the data analyzed? Using Python and the pandas library, I executed a recursive search to consolidate over 1.7 million rows of Form 4 Non-Derivative Transactions (spanning 2020–2025). The analysis involved inner-merging transaction datasets with submission metadata on their unique accession numbers. I calculated the "Reporting Lag" by subtracting the transaction date from the filing date, and then binned the data into congressional wealth brackets to analyze compliance rates across different economic tiers.

How is the data used? This analysis is used to interrogate the temporal asymmetry of financial markets. Rather than using the data to predict stock prices (seeking "alpha"), this project uses the data to expose how the 48-hour regulatory delay operates as a mechanism for structural inequality.


Data Audit

A technical analysis of the 1.7 million row dataset reveals deep structural flaws in the SEC's collection and verification architecture.

Data Cleaning & Preprocessing

To compute the reporting lag, the fragmented data had to be standardized. The steps included:

  • Consolidation: Recursive glob searches to combine scattered quarterly TSV files into a single dataframe.
  • Date Coercion: Using pd.to_datetime(errors='coerce') to force inconsistent SEC string formats into datetime objects.
  • Missing Data & Anomalies: Dropping entries with "NaT" (Not a Time) values and filtering out negative lags (which represent logical impossibilities or clerical errors).
  • Variable Creation: Multiplying shares by price-per-share to create a total Value column, then categorizing trades into specific wealth brackets (e.g., <$1k, $500k-$1M, >$1M).

Data Cleaning Reflection

The Bias of "Clean" Data: When standardizing the dates to calculate the reporting lag, my Python script utilized the errors='coerce' function. This automatically converted unparseable or severely corrupted date strings into null values, effectively dropping them from the analysis. While this is standard data science practice, from a Data Feminist perspective, it functions as a form of digital erasure.

By dropping the messiest data to create a clean statistical model, the algorithm introduces a severe Survivorship Bias. It naturally hides the most evasive, unaccountable actors. It rewards "neat" reporting and silences the chaotic data that often characterizes the most predatory corners of the market. This reflection highlighted a critical limitation: by optimizing for computational legibility, my own oversight algorithm unintentionally reinforced the privacy of the powerful.


Visualization Analysis

The following visualizations were generated from the cleaned SEC dataset to expose the mechanics of temporal asymmetry.

Visualization 1: The Compliance Paradox

Figure 1: Percentage of Trades with Delayed Disclosure (>2 Days) by Transaction Size.

What story does it tell?

This visualization reveals what I term the "Compliance Paradox." The highest-wealth insiders (executing trades >$1M) possess the highest rates of perfect compliance with the 48-hour rule, while lower-value trades experience significantly higher rates of administrative delay. It tells the story of compliance as an institutional luxury. The "Data Elite" use expensive legal teams and automated software to master the algorithmic deadline.

What story does it hide?

By showing that the elite are "compliant," the chart hides the material harm of that compliance. It obscures the fact that a 48-hour delay for a $50 million trade is legally acceptable, yet devastating to the market. The binary of "delayed vs. on-time" implies that on-time trades are inherently harmless, failing to capture how the elite use the law as a shield to legitimize information hoarding.

Visualization 2: Macro Harm and Situated Knowledge

Figure 2: Insider Trading Volume vs. COVID-19 Market Crash Timeline (Early 2020).

What story does it tell?

This chart maps insider trading volume against the March 2020 COVID-19 crash. It shows a massive spike in insider sell-offs starting nearly three weeks before the National Emergency was declared. It tells a story of Situated Knowledges (Haraway, 1988): insiders possessed highly specific, localized knowledge of the impending economic shutdown, and used the SEC's 48-hour reporting lag as a buffer to secure their wealth while the public was assured of stability.

What story does it hide?

The visualization hides the faces of the victims. A line chart showing trading volume cannot depict the teachers' pension funds, the union retirement accounts, or the middle-class 401(k)s that ultimately absorbed the mathematical aftershocks of this massive capital flight. It visualizes the extraction, but abstracts the poverty it causes.


Algorithm Logic Analysis

Decision-Making Process

The Regulatory Algorithm: The SEC's reporting system operates on a binary logic based on a rigid 48-hour window. The algorithm is optimized for institutional legitimacy rather than real-time public accountability. By hardcoding a 48-hour delay into the law, the system structurally determines that the public does not "need" to know about a trade until two days after the material impact has already occurred.

Intersectional Harm Documentation

When algorithmic delay and institutional power converge, the result is the systemic extraction of wealth from the working class. Through the lens of Data Feminism (Examining Power), the 48-hour delay ceases to be a neutral administrative variable.

Harm Analysis 1: The Automation of Structural Poverty

The material consequences of this system are borne by the American middle class. Most average citizens participate in the market through 401(k) plans and mutual funds, which operate on slower, public-facing cycles. Because the regulatory algorithm grants a two-day window of privacy, insiders liquidate millions before the public is aware. By the time the data reaches a retirement fund manager, the "alpha" has already been extracted by high-frequency algorithms. This hierarchy ensures the public is always buying at the peak and selling at the trough, effectively automating poverty.

Connection to readings: This perfectly illustrates D'Ignazio and Klein’s mandate to Examine Power. The algorithm treats the 48-hour reporting lag as a neutral variable, rather than a deliberate tool for wealth concentration.

Harm Analysis 2: The Facade of Objective Legitimacy

By defining "clean" and "compliant" data purely by a temporal timestamp (Did it arrive within 48 hours?), the system forces chaotic, highly political human behavior into a sterile binary. An algorithm fed this data validates a massive insider pre-shock sell-off as routine and legitimate simply because the paperwork was filed on time. It grants a "Privacy of Complexity" to the most unaccountable actors.

Connection to readings: This reflects the danger of failing to Challenge Binaries. The binary of on-time vs. delayed completely erases the situated context of the trade, allowing algorithmic trading platforms to commodify the data without interrogating its moral hazard.
