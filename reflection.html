Reflection & Future Directions

Synthesis, implications, and lessons learned

Synthesis of Findings

This project began as a data audit of SEC Form 4 filings, but ultimately revealed how administrative algorithms engineer economic disparity. By applying Data Feminist principles to financial disclosure, I synthesized three major insights about the mechanics of institutional power.

About the Problem

The 48-hour "Reported Silence" is not a neutral administrative buffer; it is a legally sanctioned mechanism for wealth extraction. The data proves that insiders utilize their situated knowledge to secure capital before the public is aware, effectively shifting the risk of market volatility onto the retirement funds of the working class.

About the System

The regulatory algorithm of the SEC optimizes for institutional legitimacy, not public accountability. My audit revealed a "Compliance Paradox," where the highest-wealth insiders (executing >$1M trades) are the most compliant. They use the law as a shield, employing automated infrastructure to make market-destabilizing capital flights appear perfectly routine.

About Alternatives

Counter-data platforms like Refinitiv's StarMine do not solve this asymmetry; they commodify it. By charging hedge funds for ultra-low latency feeds, they enclose public transparency behind a corporate paywall. A true feminist redesign must shift from post-trade documentation to a pre-trade escrow, equalizing latency for all stakeholders.


Key Takeaways

1

Transparency Without Speed is Wealth Extraction

If an average person’s 401(k) or pension fund only receives information after the corporate elite and high-frequency algorithms have already traded on it, then the transparency isn't serving the public—it is simply a receipt documenting how they were cheated.

2

"Clean Data" Hides Systemic Failure

Standard data science practices—like dropping corrupted date strings to make a model compute—introduce a dangerous survivorship bias. By optimizing for computational legibility, we inadvertently sanitize the audit and hide the most evasive, unaccountable actors in the financial system.

3

Compliance is an Institutional Luxury

Perfect compliance with the rules does not equate to ethical behavior. The "Data Elite" have captured the regulatory infrastructure, ensuring the rules are built to accommodate their speed while penalizing smaller actors for administrative friction.


Implications

For Policy & Practice

This research demands a fundamental shift in how the SEC structures its data architecture. Financial regulators must stop acting as passive repositories and begin acting as active gatekeepers.

  • Pre-Trade Public Escrows: Implementing a mandatory 48-hour delay on massive insider trades before execution, rather than after.
  • Latency Equalization: Introducing algorithmic "speed bumps" on institutional APIs to ensure retail investors and hedge funds receive data simultaneously.
  • Automated Point-of-Entry Verification: Rejecting impossible data inputs (e.g., negative lags) at the time of submission to preserve data integrity.

For Affected Communities

The primary victims of this system are middle-class workers whose economic resilience is tied to slower institutional funds.

  • Empowers labor unions and pension fund managers to demand fairer reporting cycles to protect their members' retirement wealth.
  • Provides concrete evidence of how systemic precarity is manufactured, shifting the blame of portfolio losses from "bad market luck" to targeted institutional extraction.

For Data Justice

This project proves that the formatting and timing of data are profoundly political.

  • Demonstrates that "making data public" is insufficient if the format (fragmented TSV files) creates insurmountable technical friction.
  • Validates feminist refusal as a core technical methodology—specifically, refusing to build predictive algorithms that gamify the surveillance of low-wealth individuals.


Limitations

A rigorous Data Feminist approach requires acknowledging the boundaries and limitations of the research itself.

Data Limitations

  • The SEC database completely lacks point-of-entry verification. I was forced to rely on self-reported data authored by the very individuals I was auditing.
  • Crucial context is missing. The data shows when a trade occurred, but not the explicit intent or backroom conversations that motivated it, flattening human behavior into a clinical metric.

Methodological Limitations

  • To make 1.7 million rows computable, my Python script relied on error coercion (`errors='coerce'`). This inherently dropped corrupted data points, creating a statistical model that likely underestimates the true scale of extreme reporting delays.
  • Flattening a complex relational database into a linear table forced me to strip away qualitative footnotes, losing the nuances of complex equity compensation packages.

Scope Limitations

  • This project only addresses traditional equity trades logged on Form 4. It does not address capital flight into dark pools, complex derivatives, or offshore shell corporations—avenues the elite would inevitably exploit if the Pre-Trade Escrow were implemented.
  • The audit focuses strictly on domestic U.S. markets and does not analyze the downstream effects of U.S. market volatility on the global economy.


Future Directions

Research Questions

Question 1: The Pension Fund Impact

What is the exact, quantifiable dollar amount lost by major union pension funds (e.g., CalPERS) during the 48-hour "Reported Silence" preceding major market crashes?

Why it matters: Tying the abstract "Reporting Lag" to specific, local economic losses is crucial for mobilizing political momentum for regulatory reform.

Question 2: Institutional Evasion Tactics

If a $1M Pre-Trade Escrow threshold were implemented, what mathematical models would high-frequency trading firms develop to split massive trades and evade the trigger?

Why it matters: Regulatory algorithms must anticipate adversarial adaptation. Understanding how capital flows around friction is essential for resilient data design.

Methodological Improvements

  • Natural Language Processing (NLP): Integrating an NLP model to parse and categorize the qualitative footnotes attached to SEC filings, recovering the contextual nuance lost in pure quantitative merges.
  • Blockchain Integration: Cross-referencing SEC filings with crypto wallet movements to detect if insiders use decentralized platforms to hedge against their public equity positions during the 48-hour silence.

Action Steps

  • Open Source Tooling: Publish a clean, consolidated version of the 2020-2025 SEC dataset on an open repository (like Harvard Dataverse) to relieve future researchers of the "invisible labor" of TSV consolidation.
  • Policy Advocacy: Draft a white paper based on the Pre-Trade Escrow redesign to submit to the SEC's Investor Advisory Committee.


Personal Reflection

This project represents a critical convergence of my academic training and my commitment to data justice.

What I Learned About...

The Research Process & Technical Skills

Processing 1.7 million rows of fragmented SEC data pushed my technical capabilities beyond standard classroom datasets. I mastered complex recursive `pandas` operations, inner-merging massive relational datasets, and handling extreme standard deviations. More importantly, I learned how technical decisions shape political outcomes. Recognizing that my own use of standard error-handling code effectively erased the messiest actors from the audit was a profound realization about the invisible biases embedded in "clean" data science.

My Own Positionality

In my quantitative coursework, correctness is traditionally defined by mathematical convergence and statistical validity. Simultaneously, my experience managing the business operations and investigative reporting of a student publication has taught me that financial records are rarely neutral—they are narratives of power. This project forced me to bridge those two worlds. I could not treat these 1.7 million rows as abstract variables; I had to approach them as documents of economic precarity. Analyzing this system shifted my perspective, proving that having the technical capacity to model data is insufficient if you do not also have the critical lens to ask who that data serves.

The Subject Matter

I began this project viewing transparency as the ultimate solution to financial inequality. I am concluding it with the understanding that transparency without speed is just an archive of extraction. True data justice requires optimizing systems for the protection of the vulnerable, rather than the velocity of the powerful.


Conclusion

Final Reflection: The automation of structural inequality does not happen in the shadows; it happens in plain sight, protected by the illusion of perfect compliance. By auditing the SEC's 48-hour "Reported Silence," this project demonstrates that financial algorithms and disclosure laws are fundamentally rigged to grant the institutional elite a temporal monopoly on actionable knowledge. We cannot code our way out of this inequality simply by making the data cleaner or building faster predictive models. True data justice requires a feminist refusal of extractive systems. We must redesign our digital infrastructures to ensure that information precedes capital, dismantling the temporal monopolies of the powerful to protect the economic resilience of the public.
